CHAPTER PREPARATION PROMPT (STRICT FORMAT – CONTROLLED OUTPUT)

Prompt #1: Structural Blueprint

I am preparing to write the [Chapter Name] chapter of a non-fiction book built around a governing thesis and structural analysis.

Its structure:
[Structure]

Do NOT write the chapter. Do NOT provide sample prose. Do NOT simulate examples. Do NOT include hypothetical theses. Do NOT expand beyond requested structure.

Your output must contain exactly THREE sections, in this order, using bullet points only. Maximum 250 words per section.

SECTION 1 – PURPOSE AND STRUCTURAL DESIGN
Provide:
The intellectual purpose of this type of Introduction.
The cognitive shift it must produce in the reader.
The structural architecture required to achieve that purpose.
The functional role of each structural segment.
No narrative explanation. Bullet points only.

SECTION 2 – REQUIRED CONTENT AND CHRONOLOGICAL ORDER
Provide:
The exact content elements that must appear.
The correct chronological sequence.
A short justification (1 sentence maximum per point) for why each must appear in that position.
List in numbered order only.

SECTION 3 – INFORMATION ACQUISITION FRAMEWORK
For each major content element above, specify:
What I must ask myself.
What I must ask others (if relevant).
Where to gather evidence (specific source categories only).
Format as structured bullets. No abstraction. No philosophy. No examples.

Terminate immediately after Section 3.

Prompt #2: Writing & Analysis Guide

Please return to Section 2 of your previous response and guide me on how to write all the required content.

Do not write the actual content. Instead, explain:
What must be written
How it must be written
The structural logic governing each part

For every component, provide one concise demonstration example strictly for modeling purposes.

Then, analyze that example and explain why it functions as a well-crafted model. Your analysis must be detailed, technical, and non-generic. Avoid surface-level commentary.

Your analysis must explicitly address:
How the sentences are structured (clause design, information sequencing, emphasis placement, abstraction level, rhythm, logical progression)
The ideal sentence types and voice, including why those choices are structurally appropriate
How the structural design enables the example to serve its intended purpose
How each individual sentence contributes functionally to that purpose

Afterward, conduct a sentence-by-sentence audit of every example using the following mandatory analytical framework. No section may be omitted, generalized, or answered briefly.

Sentence Structure:
 Sentence 1:
 Sentence 2:
 Sentence n:
 (Break down clause composition, logical ordering, emphasis mechanics, and structural intent.)

Type & Voice:
 (Specify sentence type, grammatical construction, and voice. Explain why these choices are structurally optimal.)

Purpose Served:
 (Define the exact functional objective of the passage.)

Structural Requirements to Serve the Purpose:
 (Explicitly define what must be done structurally — ordering logic, informational hierarchy, constraint framing, abstraction control, pacing, emphasis — to ensure the purpose is achieved.)

Sentence Effectiveness:
 (Evaluate how each sentence operationalizes the structural requirement.)

Guideline:
 (Extract a precise, repeatable structural principle derived from the analysis. It must be actionable, not abstract.)

Maintain analytical precision. Avoid motivational tone. Avoid generic praise. Avoid vague statements such as “clear,” “strong,” or “effective” without structural justification. Every claim must be structurally explained.
